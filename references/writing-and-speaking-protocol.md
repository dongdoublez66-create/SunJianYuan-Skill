# Writing And Speaking Protocol

## Scope

Use this protocol to produce style-aligned scientific prose and talk tracks. The source corpus is mainly published papers, so treat "speaking style" as an inferred academic presentation voice unless the user provides talks, interviews, grant defenses, or meeting transcripts.

Do not impersonate Sun Jianyuan or claim authorship. Do not copy distinctive sentences. Rebuild the content in the user's own scientific context using the observed rhetorical habits, claim calibration, and terminology density.

## Diagnostic Pass

Before rewriting, identify:

1. The biological level: molecule, synapse, circuit, behavior, disease, or method.
2. The evidence type: electrophysiology, structural biology, biosensing, animal model, clinical tissue, review synthesis, or method development.
3. The claim status: direct observation, statistical association, mechanistic inference, model, or speculation.
4. The manuscript target: abstract, introduction, results, discussion, cover letter, response to reviewers, grant-style summary, or oral presentation.
5. The risk of overclaiming: mark any causal or translational claim that is not directly supported.

## Core Style Moves

Use these moves in order when appropriate:

1. Establish the process or system with precise technical nouns.
2. State the unresolved mechanistic problem in a restrained way.
3. Introduce the experimental or analytical approach without flourish.
4. Report the key observation with direct evidence verbs.
5. Interpret the observation cautiously when moving beyond direct data.
6. Close with a mechanistic or conceptual contribution, not a broad promotional claim.

## Abstract Pattern

Prefer this compact arc:

- Sentence 1: Define the biological process, clinical problem, or method domain.
- Sentence 2: State the missing mechanism, controversy, or measurement limitation.
- Sentence 3: "Here" or "In this study" move, naming the system and approach.
- Sentences 4-6: Key findings in a direct, evidence-led sequence.
- Final sentence: Contribution framed as "provide insight", "reveal a mechanism", "support a model", or "offer an approach"; avoid inflated impact language.

## Introduction Pattern

Build from field to mechanism:

- Paragraph 1: Biological or clinical importance, with restraint.
- Paragraph 2: Known mechanistic components and the gap between them.
- Paragraph 3: Why the chosen model, method, or structure can resolve the gap.
- Final paragraph: Study objective, concise method overview, and expected conceptual payoff.

Do not overfill the introduction with broad neuroscience background. Keep it close to the mechanistic problem.

## Results Pattern

Keep result paragraphs evidence-first:

- Start with the question or assay purpose.
- Name the experimental condition, construct, sensor, circuit, or preparation.
- Give the observation.
- Add statistical or comparative interpretation.
- End with what the result supports, not a full discussion claim.

Use assertive verbs for measured results: show, reveal, demonstrate, identify, establish, support.
Use caution for interpretation: suggest, indicate, may, could, likely, potential.

## Discussion Pattern

Use a layered synthesis:

- First paragraph: Recap the main mechanistic advance in one restrained sentence, then connect findings.
- Middle paragraphs: Explain how each result changes the model, resolves a gap, or constrains interpretation.
- Limitations: State them plainly when needed; avoid apologetic phrasing.
- Final paragraph: Return to mechanism and future investigation; avoid grand endings.

## Sentence And Tone Rules

- Prefer clear, technical sentences around 15-25 words, with occasional longer sentences for multi-part mechanisms.
- Use active voice when describing the study's actions and findings.
- Use passive voice sparingly for standard methods or accepted field knowledge.
- Use transitions such as however, in addition, in contrast, together, and collectively when they genuinely mark logic.
- Avoid decorative adjectives, dramatic metaphors, marketing language, and vague excellence claims.
- Keep terms stable: do not alternate synonyms when a technical term should remain fixed.

## Speaking / Presentation Voice

For slides, chalk talks, lab meetings, and oral summaries:

- Convert the paper logic into a three-part spoken arc: problem, mechanism, evidence.
- Use short signposts: "The key question is...", "To test this...", "This result suggests...".
- Keep claims measured and data-bound.
- Explain methods only to the depth needed to understand the inference.
- End each result slide with the mechanistic takeaway.
- Avoid casual jokes or rhetorical drama unless the user explicitly asks for a lighter style.

### Evidence Boundary For Speaking

The current corpus is written-paper dominant. Therefore, generate an inferred academic reporting voice, not a full personal imitation. A full spoken-style model requires the user to provide recordings, transcripts, interviews, teaching clips, conference talks, or Q&A notes.

If speech samples are provided, first extract:

- recurring opening and transition phrases;
- sentence length in spoken Chinese or English;
- how uncertainty is expressed aloud;
- whether the speaker uses first-person singular, first-person plural, or impersonal framing;
- how figures are introduced;
- how questions are answered when the evidence is incomplete;
- whether summaries are compressed or repeated for emphasis.

Only after this extraction should the skill claim to reflect real speaking habits.

### Inferred Oral Style Profile

Use this oral style when no speech transcript is available:

- Tone: calm, technical, slightly compressed, and data-bound.
- Persona: a PI or senior scientist explaining the logic of the experiment, not a storyteller promoting a result.
- Default stance: "This result supports a mechanism" rather than "This proves a broad conclusion."
- Rhythm: one short framing sentence, one method sentence, one observation sentence, then one interpretation sentence.
- Audience relation: assume the audience understands neuroscience but still needs the causal chain made explicit.
- Emphasis: emphasize the question, preparation, measured signal, and mechanistic implication.
- Avoid: motivational opening, dramatic contrast, autobiographical detail, jokes, and broad claims about clinical impact.

### Talk Opening Pattern

Use a restrained opening. Do not start with a grand field statement unless the user asks for a public-facing talk.

Preferred pattern:

1. "今天我主要汇报一个问题..."
2. "这个问题和...有关，但关键机制还不完全清楚。"
3. "我们最近从...三个层面做了一些实验。"
4. "我会先讲现象，再讲机制上的解释。"

Example skeleton:

"今天我主要汇报最近关于 [process] 的几组结果。这个过程对 [function] 很重要，但 [mechanistic gap] 还没有完全解决。我们从 [method/preparation 1]、[method/preparation 2] 和 [circuit/behavioral level] 三个层面做了分析。下面我先讲观察到的现象，再讨论它可能支持的机制。"

### Slide-Level Speaking Pattern

For each slide or result block, use this sequence:

1. Question: "这一页想回答的是..."
2. Method: "我们用...来测量..."
3. Observation: "可以看到..."
4. Control or comparison: "相比之下..." or "在...条件下..."
5. Inference: "这提示..."
6. Boundary: "但这个结果还不能说明..."

Compact slide narration skeleton:

"这一页主要看 [question]。我们在 [preparation] 中用 [method] 记录 [signal]。可以看到，在 [condition] 下，[observation]。相比对照组，[comparison]。这个结果提示 [mechanistic inference]，但目前还不能区分 [alternative explanation A] 和 [alternative explanation B]。"

### Mechanism Explanation Pattern

When explaining a mechanism aloud, keep the causal chain explicit:

1. "如果把这个过程拆开..."
2. "第一步是..."
3. "第二步涉及..."
4. "我们的数据主要支持的是..."
5. "还没有直接证明的是..."

Example skeleton:

"如果把这个过程拆开，第一步是 [upstream event]，第二步是 [synaptic/cellular event]，最后才表现为 [output]。我们的数据主要支持 [specific link]，因为 [evidence]。但是 [unmeasured step] 还没有被直接记录，所以这里更合适的说法是 [cautious interpretation]。"

### Result Interpretation Language

Use these spoken phrases for calibrated certainty:

- Direct observation: "我们看到的是...", "这个变化比较稳定", "这个结果在两个条件下都能重复。"
- Supported inference: "这提示...", "比较合理的解释是...", "这更支持...这个模型。"
- Open uncertainty: "但这里还有一个问题", "现在还不能排除...", "这个需要后面进一步验证。"
- Transition to next experiment: "所以接下来我们问...", "为了区分这两种可能性...", "下一步我们把这个问题放到...条件下看。"

Avoid saying:

- "这证明了..."
- "这是决定性的机制..."
- "完全解释了..."
- "首次全面揭示..."

unless the user's data directly support such wording and the target context expects stronger language.

### Group Meeting Update Pattern

For a brief lab meeting progress report:

1. Start with the question, not the background.
2. State what was done since the last update.
3. Separate robust observations from unstable or preliminary results.
4. End with the next experiment and what decision it will enable.

Template:

"这周主要推进的是 [question]。我们完成了 [experiment/analysis]，目前比较稳定的结果是 [observation]。有一个现象还不够稳定，就是 [unstable point]，可能和 [technical or biological variable] 有关。下一步我会先做 [control/replicate]，如果这个结果成立，就可以进一步判断 [mechanistic decision]。"

### Q&A Response Pattern

When answering questions, do not over-defend. Use a measured answer:

1. Acknowledge the question.
2. State what the current data do and do not show.
3. Name the experiment that would resolve the uncertainty.

Template:

"这是一个很关键的问题。现在的数据能说明 [supported point]，但还不能直接说明 [unsupported point]。如果要区分这两种可能性，我们需要在 [condition] 下直接测 [signal/variable]。所以目前我会把它表述为 [cautious conclusion]。"

### Chinese Oral Register

For Chinese reports, prefer natural academic Chinese rather than translated English. Use compact linking phrases:

- "这里的问题是..."
- "我们先看..."
- "可以看到..."
- "这个结果提示..."
- "但这里要谨慎一点..."
- "接下来我们想区分..."
- "所以这一部分的小结是..."

Keep English technical terms when they are standard in Chinese lab speech: synaptic vesicle, Ca2+ channel, release probability, endocytosis, postsynaptic current, sensor, circuit, input, output.

### English Oral Register

For English talks, use direct scientific signposting:

- "The key question here is..."
- "To test this, we measured..."
- "What we found is..."
- "This result suggests..."
- "However, this does not yet distinguish..."
- "Therefore, the next step is..."

Avoid overly polished keynote rhetoric. Use conference/lab-meeting clarity.

## Revision Checklist

Before returning a draft, check:

- Does every major claim have the right certainty level?
- Are mechanisms and evidence clearly separated?
- Are technical nouns more prominent than adjectives?
- Is the final sentence restrained and contribution-focused?
- Are there any copied phrases longer than a short generic expression?
- Is the user's actual science preserved rather than forced into the corpus topics?
