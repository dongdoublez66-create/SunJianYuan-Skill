# Writing And Speaking Notes

These notes turn the corpus profile into practical writing moves. They are not a script to impersonate Sun Jianyuan. They are a way to keep the same kind of scientific discipline: mechanism first, evidence second, interpretation carefully bounded.

## First Pass

Before rewriting anything, quietly sort the material:

- What level is the work at: molecule, synapse, circuit, behavior, disease, structure, or method?
- What kind of evidence is being used: electrophysiology, imaging, structural biology, biosensing, animal behavior, clinical tissue, review synthesis, or method development?
- Which statements are direct observations, and which are mechanistic interpretations?
- What is the target form: abstract, introduction, result paragraph, discussion, reviewer response, slide narration, or spoken update?
- Where could the text overclaim?

This first pass matters more than surface wording. The style comes from how the argument is controlled.

## The Basic Move

A typical paragraph or spoken block should move like this:

1. Establish the process or preparation.
2. Name the unresolved mechanistic point.
3. Say what was measured or compared.
4. State the observation directly.
5. Interpret it with the right level of caution.
6. End with what the result adds to the model.

The writing should feel precise rather than dramatic. Let the verbs do the work.

## Abstracts

A strong abstract usually has a compact arc:

- Begin with the biological process or measurement problem.
- State the gap without making it sound mysterious for its own sake.
- Introduce the study with "Here" or "In this study" only when it helps.
- Put the main findings in a clean evidence sequence.
- End with a contribution such as "provide insight", "support a model", "reveal a mechanism", or "offer an approach".

Avoid endings that sound like a press release. If the data support a mechanism, say that. If they suggest one, keep the verb soft.

## Introductions

Move from field to mechanism, not from field to field.

A useful shape:

- Paragraph 1: why the process matters.
- Paragraph 2: what is known about the components.
- Paragraph 3: what connection remains unclear.
- Final paragraph: what the study tests and why the chosen system can answer it.

Keep the general neuroscience background short. The introduction should bring the reader toward the specific mechanistic uncertainty.

## Results

A result paragraph should usually answer one question.

Good order:

- "We asked whether..."
- "To test this, we measured..."
- "We found..."
- "Compared with..."
- "These data suggest/support..."

Use stronger verbs for measured results: show, reveal, demonstrate, identify, support.

Use softer verbs for interpretation: suggest, indicate, may, could, likely, potential.

Keep the result paragraph from turning into discussion too early.

## Discussion

The discussion should gather the results into a model, not simply repeat them.

Start by stating the main mechanistic advance in one restrained sentence. Then explain how each result changes the model, resolves a gap, or narrows the possible explanations. State limitations plainly. End by returning to the mechanism and the next experiment, not by making a grand claim.

Useful discussion language:

- "Together, these results support..."
- "One interpretation is..."
- "This model is consistent with..."
- "However, this does not exclude..."
- "Future experiments will need to determine..."

## Sentence Tone

Prefer sentences that are clear and technical, often around 15 to 25 words. Longer sentences are fine when they carry a mechanistic chain, but they should not become decorative.

Good habits:

- Keep terms stable instead of rotating synonyms.
- Use active voice for what the study did or found.
- Use passive voice for standard methods or field knowledge when it sounds natural.
- Use transitions only when they mark real logic: however, in addition, in contrast, together, collectively.
- Avoid adjectives that mainly advertise importance.

## Speaking: What We Can Infer

The current material is paper-heavy. Without real talks or transcripts, the safest speaking style is an inferred academic reporting voice:

- calm and technical;
- compressed but not rushed;
- focused on the question, preparation, measurement, and interpretation;
- careful about what is shown versus what is inferred;
- closer to a lab meeting or conference update than to a popular talk.

If actual speech samples are provided later, study them separately: openings, transition phrases, self-corrections, pacing, figure descriptions, and how uncertain answers are handled. Only then should the speaking style be treated as personal rather than inferred.

## Opening A Report

Avoid a broad motivational flourish unless the audience is non-specialist. Start with the question.

Natural Chinese opening:

"今天我主要汇报最近关于 [process] 的几组结果。这个过程和 [function] 有关，但其中 [mechanistic gap] 还不完全清楚。我们从 [preparation/method 1]、[method 2] 和 [circuit/behavioral level] 三个层面做了一些实验。下面我先讲现象，再讲它可能支持的机制。"

Shorter group-meeting opening:

"这周主要推进的是 [question]。比较稳定的结果有两个，另外还有一个现象需要再验证。"

## Explaining One Slide

Each slide should make one point. A useful rhythm is:

1. 这一页想回答的是...
2. 我们用...来测量...
3. 可以看到...
4. 相比之下...
5. 这个结果提示...
6. 但目前还不能区分...

Example:

"这一页主要看 [question]。我们在 [preparation] 中用 [method] 记录 [signal]。可以看到，在 [condition] 下，[observation]。相比对照组，[comparison]。这个结果提示 [mechanistic inference]，但目前还不能区分 [alternative A] 和 [alternative B]。"

## Explaining A Mechanism Aloud

Keep the causal chain visible. A good spoken explanation often sounds like:

"如果把这个过程拆开，第一步是 [upstream event]，第二步是 [synaptic/cellular event]，最后才表现为 [output]。我们的数据主要支持 [specific link]，因为 [evidence]。但是 [unmeasured step] 还没有被直接记录，所以这里更合适的说法是 [cautious interpretation]。"

This style is not dramatic. It is useful because it tells the audience where the evidence stops.

## Interpreting Results In A Talk

Good spoken phrases:

- "我们看到的是..."
- "这个变化比较稳定。"
- "这个结果在两个条件下都能重复。"
- "这提示..."
- "比较合理的解释是..."
- "现在还不能排除..."
- "为了区分这两种可能性..."
- "下一步我们会直接测..."

Avoid these unless the data truly justify them:

- "这证明了..."
- "这是决定性的机制..."
- "完全解释了..."
- "首次全面揭示..."

## Group Meeting Updates

For a short progress report, keep it practical:

"这周主要推进的是 [question]。我们完成了 [experiment/analysis]，目前比较稳定的结果是 [observation]。有一个现象还不够稳定，就是 [unstable point]，可能和 [technical or biological variable] 有关。下一步我会先做 [control/replicate]，如果这个结果成立，就可以进一步判断 [mechanistic decision]。"

This format is useful because it separates progress, uncertainty, and the next decision.

## Answering Questions

A measured answer is usually better than over-defending a result:

"这是一个很关键的问题。现在的数据能说明 [supported point]，但还不能直接说明 [unsupported point]。如果要区分这两种可能性，我们需要在 [condition] 下直接测 [signal/variable]。所以目前我会把它表述为 [cautious conclusion]。"

The important habit is to name both the supported point and the missing experiment.

## Chinese Register

For Chinese reports, keep the language natural for a lab or seminar. English technical terms can stay in the sentence when they are normally used that way:

- synaptic vesicle
- Ca2+ channel
- release probability
- endocytosis
- postsynaptic current
- sensor
- circuit
- input/output

Useful connectors:

- "这里的问题是..."
- "我们先看..."
- "可以看到..."
- "这个结果提示..."
- "但这里要谨慎一点..."
- "接下来我们想区分..."
- "所以这一部分的小结是..."

## English Register

For English talks, keep the signposts direct:

- "The key question here is..."
- "To test this, we measured..."
- "What we found is..."
- "This result suggests..."
- "However, this does not yet distinguish..."
- "Therefore, the next step is..."

Avoid keynote-style rhetoric unless the user asks for it. The default should sound like a scientist explaining data to other scientists.

## Last Check

Before returning a draft, check whether:

- the certainty level is right;
- mechanisms and measurements are separated;
- technical nouns carry the paragraph;
- the final sentence is restrained;
- no source-paper sentence has been copied;
- the user's actual science has not been forced into the corpus topics.
